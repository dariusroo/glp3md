Eli Lilly Retatrutide Expanded Access: Is There a Patient Access Program Before FDA Approval?

April 24, 2026  ·  6 min read

Is There an Eli Lilly Retatrutide Patient Access Program or Expanded Access?

Retatrutide — commonly referred to as reta in patient communities — has generated extraordinary anticipation. After the TRIUMPH-4 Phase 3 trial reported a mean weight loss of 28.7% over 68 weeks at the 12mg dose, the highest ever recorded in a Phase 3 obesity trial, patients and clinicians alike are asking the same urgent question: is there any way to access retatrutide before FDA approval? Specifically, many are searching for an Eli Lilly retatrutide patient access program or an expanded access pathway. The short answer requires careful unpacking.

This post explains the regulatory landscape honestly, without shortcuts or false promises, so patients can make informed decisions about their next steps.

Is Retatrutide FDA Approved and Available to Prescribe Today?

As of mid-2025, retatrutide is not FDA approved. No New Drug Application (NDA) has been filed with the FDA as of April 2026, and no formal approval timeline has been publicly confirmed by Eli Lilly. That means retatrutide cannot be legally prescribed by any physician in the United States through ordinary commercial channels.

It is worth putting the clinical stakes in perspective. The Phase 2 trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine showed that participants receiving the 8mg dose achieved up to 22.8% mean weight loss at 48 weeks, with 100% of participants in that arm losing at least 5% of their body weight. The TRIUMPH-4 Phase 3 data elevated those figures even further. For context:

Drug Mechanism Trial Duration Mean Weight Loss (Highest Dose)
Semaglutide (Wegovy) GLP-1 agonist 68 weeks (STEP-1) ~14.9%
Tirzepatide (Zepbound) GLP-1 / GIP dual agonist 72 weeks (SURMOUNT-1) ~20.9% at 15mg
Retatrutide GLP-1 / GIP / glucagon triple agonist 68 weeks (TRIUMPH-4) 28.7% at 12mg

The glucagon receptor component is what distinguishes retatrutide as a true triple agonist. Beyond food noise reduction — that persistent, intrusive preoccupation with eating that GLP-1 medications are known to quiet — the glucagon arm adds meaningful energy expenditure effects and favorable cardiometabolic changes including significant reductions in triglycerides and LDL cholesterol observed in Phase 2 data.

Can Patients Access Retatrutide Through a Clinical Trial Before Approval?

Clinical trials represent one legitimate pre-approval access pathway. The Phase 2 obesity trial (NCT04881760) enrolled 338 participants across multiple U.S. sites and has been completed. The TRIUMPH Phase 3 program, which produced the landmark 28.7% weight loss figure, has also closed enrollment — no new patient slots are available through those studies.

Patients should be aware that participating in a clinical trial is not equivalent to receiving a treatment prescription. Trial participation involves randomization (meaning some participants receive placebo), strict eligibility criteria, scheduled site visits, and protocol-defined procedures. The Phase 2 trial, for example, excluded individuals with type 2 diabetes, prior bariatric surgery, prior GLP-1 receptor agonist use, and body weight changes greater than 5kg in the preceding three months. Phase 3 trials carry similarly rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria.

If new trials open in the future — such as trials examining retatrutide in populations with type 2 diabetes or cardiovascular disease — those would be listed on ClinicalTrials.gov. Patients interested in research participation should monitor that registry directly.

What Is the Difference Between Expanded Access and a Clinical Trial for Retatrutide?

These two pathways are frequently confused, and the distinction matters enormously.

  • Clinical trial: A structured research protocol in which participants are enrolled under an Investigational New Drug (IND) application, randomized to treatment arms, and followed according to a predetermined schedule. Patients contribute data to the scientific record.
  • Expanded access (compassionate use): A separate FDA regulatory mechanism that allows individual patients or small groups to receive an investigational drug outside of a clinical trial, typically when the patient has a serious or life-threatening condition and no comparable alternatives exist.

Expanded access requires a physician to submit an IND application to the FDA and obtain authorization before administering the drug. The drug manufacturer — in this case, Eli Lilly — must also agree to provide the medication. Eli Lilly has not announced a formal expanded access program for retatrutide as of mid-2025. Without manufacturer participation and FDA authorization, no legitimate expanded access pathway exists. There are no shortcuts to this process.

FDA expanded access is not a routine channel and is generally reserved for conditions where existing approved therapies have failed and the medical situation is serious. Obesity, while a recognized chronic disease with significant morbidity, presents a complex regulatory context for compassionate use given that approved treatments do exist — even if their efficacy does not yet approach retatrutide's Phase 3 results.

Any entity claiming to offer retatrutide outside of a sanctioned clinical trial or FDA-authorized expanded access arrangement is operating outside of the law and outside the bounds of patient safety.

When Will GLP-3 Be Available Outside of Clinical Trials?

Retatrutide is frequently called a GLP-3 in patient communities as shorthand for its triple receptor mechanism. No official GLP-3 release date has been announced. The peer-reviewed Phase 3 publication from TRIUMPH-4 was still pending as of April 2026, and Eli Lilly had not yet filed an NDA with the FDA at that time.

The typical pathway from Phase 3 completion to NDA filing to FDA approval can span one to several years, depending on the speed of regulatory submissions, FDA review timelines, and any requests for additional data. Based on the strength of TRIUMPH-4's results — 28.7% mean weight loss at 68 weeks in the 12mg arm — there is broad clinical expectation that retatrutide will eventually reach patients. The question is timing, not efficacy.

When retatrutide does receive FDA approval and becomes legally prescribable in the United States, prescription access will open through licensed prescribers. Patients who are positioned and informed at that moment will be best placed to discuss candidacy with their clinical care team promptly.
