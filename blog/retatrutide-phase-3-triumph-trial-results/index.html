Retatrutide Phase 3 TRIUMPH Trial Results: What the Data Shows So Far

April 18, 2026  ·  6 min read

What Is the TRIUMPH Trial Program and What Does It Cover?

Retatrutide — commonly referred to as reta in patient communities — is a once-weekly injectable triple agonist that simultaneously activates GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors. This three-receptor mechanism distinguishes it from earlier anti-obesity medications and is the basis for the substantial weight loss figures now emerging from clinical trials. The drug is currently in Phase 3 development by Eli Lilly and Company under the compound designation LY3437943.

The TRIUMPH trial program is Eli Lilly's Phase 3 clinical development suite for retatrutide. The program encompasses multiple sub-studies evaluating the drug across a range of patient populations and clinical contexts, including adults with obesity, adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes, and populations with specific cardiovascular risk profiles. A dedicated cardiovascular outcomes trial is also included within the TRIUMPH umbrella, designed to assess whether retatrutide reduces the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events — a regulatory and clinical milestone that has become standard for obesity medications seeking broad adoption.

While peer-reviewed publications from the Phase 3 trials are still pending as of April 2026, and no New Drug Application (NDA) has yet been filed with the FDA, press release data from one key sub-study — TRIUMPH-4 — has already captured significant attention in both clinical and patient communities for delivering the highest mean weight loss ever recorded in a Phase 3 obesity trial.

What Did TRIUMPH-1 Reveal About Retatrutide's Efficacy and Safety?

TRIUMPH-1 is the pivotal efficacy and safety sub-study within the TRIUMPH program, enrolling adults with obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m²) or overweight (BMI ≥27 kg/m²) with at least one weight-related comorbidity, without type 2 diabetes. The primary endpoint measures percent total body weight loss at either 48 or 72 weeks, depending on the specific arm and design. Full peer-reviewed results from TRIUMPH-1 have not yet been published as of April 2026, and detailed efficacy and safety figures from this sub-study are not available for clinical commentary at this time.

What is already understood from the earlier Phase 2 data is that retatrutide produced weight loss outcomes that were unprecedented at the time of publication. Those findings set the scientific expectation that Phase 3 trials — conducted at larger scale, with longer duration, and with a more refined titration protocol — would be closely watched by obesity medicine specialists and patients alike.

As peer-reviewed TRIUMPH-1 data becomes available, it will be analyzed and discussed here at glp3md in full clinical detail, including primary endpoint results, responder analyses, and the safety profile at each maintenance dose.

What Is TRIUMPH-4 Studying and When Are Results Expected?

TRIUMPH-4 is the sub-study that has generated the most discussion to date. According to an Eli Lilly press release issued on December 11, 2025, TRIUMPH-4 evaluated retatrutide over a 68-week treatment period and tested both a 9mg and 12mg maintenance dose using a carefully structured titration schedule:

  • Weeks 1–4: 2mg
  • Weeks 5–8: 4mg
  • Weeks 9–12: 6mg
  • Weeks 13–16: 9mg
  • Week 17 onward: 12mg maintenance

At the 12mg dose, participants achieved a mean weight loss of 28.7% — the highest ever reported in a Phase 3 randomized controlled obesity trial. For context, this level of weight reduction begins to approach outcomes historically associated only with bariatric surgery. The magnitude of effect is consistent with retatrutide's unique glucagon receptor activity, which adds an energy expenditure component to the appetite suppression already provided by GLP-1 and GIP receptor engagement. Patients familiar with the concept of food noise — the persistent, intrusive mental preoccupation with eating — will recognize that triple agonism appears to address this on multiple physiological pathways simultaneously.

Discontinuation rates in TRIUMPH-4 were 12.2% at 9mg and 18.2% at 12mg, compared to 4.0% in the placebo group. Notably, some discontinuations at the higher dose were attributed to perceived excessive weight loss — an unusual finding in obesity pharmacotherapy trials. Dysesthesia (an abnormal skin sensation) was reported in 8.8% of 9mg participants and 20.9% of 12mg participants, compared to 0.7% in the placebo group, consistent with a dose-dependent sensory side effect signal first observed in Phase 2 data.

A peer-reviewed publication of TRIUMPH-4 results is pending as of April 2026, and no NDA filing timeline has been publicly confirmed by Eli Lilly.

How Do Phase 3 Results Compare to Phase 2 Weight Loss Findings?

The Phase 2 trial (published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2023; Jastreboff et al.; NCT04881760) established retatrutide's initial efficacy signal across four dose levels over 48 weeks in 338 participants. The Phase 3 TRIUMPH-4 data substantially extends those findings, both in duration and magnitude. The table below summarizes the comparison:

Parameter Phase 2 (48 weeks) TRIUMPH-4 Phase 3 (68 weeks)
Study duration 48 weeks 68 weeks
Highest dose tested 12mg once weekly 12mg once weekly
Mean weight loss (highest dose) ~24.2% 28.7%
≥20% weight loss achieved 64% at 12mg Not yet published (peer review pending)
≥25% weight loss achieved 48% at 12mg Not yet published (peer review pending)
Dysesthesia (highest dose) 12.9% vs 1.4% placebo 20.9% vs 0.7% placebo
Primary GI side effects Nausea, diarrhea, vomiting (dose-dependent) Consistent with Phase 2 profile
Phase of development Phase 2 Phase 3

The trajectory from Phase 2 to Phase 3 is clinically meaningful. The additional 20 weeks of treatment in TRIUMPH-4 — combined with an optimized titration schedule that includes an intermediate 6mg step — appears to have allowed more participants to reach and sustain the full 12mg maintenance dose, contributing to the incremental gain in weight loss efficacy. Phase 2 data also demonstrated consistent effects across BMI subgroups and across male and female participants, which supports the generalizability of the Phase 3 findings as they are published in full.

From a cardiometabolic standpoint, Phase 2 data showed meaningful improvements in fasting triglycerides (up to –39.3% at 8mg by week 24), LDL cholesterol (up to –16.9% at 8mg), and total cholesterol (up to –17.7% at 8mg) — markers that carry independent cardiovascular relevance beyond weight loss alone. Quality of life scores also improved across all active dose groups in Phase 2, a finding that aligns with what patients report anecdotally about reductions in food noise, fatigue, and the psychological burden of weight management.

Retatrutide is not yet FDA-approved, and the regulatory pathway — including NDA filing and review — remains ongoing.
